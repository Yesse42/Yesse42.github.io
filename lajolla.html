La Jolla's Climate

After living on UCSD's campus for around 9 months, I began to develop an intuitive sense of what the climate on campus was like: the same thing on repeat, forever and ever. That's a bit of an exaggeration, but winter and summer both feel like mother nature decided to copy-paste the same 3 days for the whole season with some minor tweaks here and there. It's always nice to have data to back up one's claims though, so I went on a quest to find some for the La Jolla area.

Luckily, there is a weather station on UCSD's Campus at SIO's Southwest Fisheries Building, and its data is freely accessible online through Synoptic Data. The downside, however, is that the station's period of record began in late 2013, which means it only has about 8 years of data. That is not ideal for the construction of an accurate climatology, for which a 30 year period of record is typically preferred. However, I can't seem to find any other nearby station with a suitable period of record, and San Diego is riddled with microclimates so even just a few miles can lead to large changes in temperature; therefore, 8 years will have to do. We just have to keep in mind that this means the averages may not be as accurate, and do some sanity checks with nearby stations to ensure nothing's gone horribly wrong.

Interpretation

Extreme Moderation

As should be apparent, UCSD's climate takes moderation to the absolute extreme. With the change in average high temperature between the coldest and warmest months standing at a mere 11ºF, and the difference in average low temperatures being 13ºF, the seasons here are muted, although not quite as nonexistent as I thought beforehand. Also note the tiny differences between average high and low temperatures in all months of the years. In August the difference between the average high and low temperature is ~6ºF! Even in wintertime the average temperature still only swings about ~9ºF each day.

The lack of cold temperatures of any sort are also rather striking, with the chilliest temperature of the last 8 years being a measly 39ºF. That's a far cry from when it snowed in La Jolla in 1967! Sadly there has been no snow since, but I still cling to a dying hope that it will one day come again. Although the nights may not be particularly cold, there have been some frigid (by San Diego standards) daytime highs where the temperature struggles to stagger beyond 50ºF. I also find it hilarious that in its 8 years of existence the station has not recorded a temperature below 60ºF in July or August.

The Pacific Ocean is the primary cause of this moderation, given the high heat capacity of the oceans and the marine layer discussed in the low clouds page. Additionally, the station's position on a steep cliff overlooking the sea moderates its wintertime overnight temperatures. This is because when the sky is clear, the air is dry, and the winds are calm (as it often is in winter), the primary cooling mechanism is the ground (not the air) radiating heat to space. This creates a layer of cool air above the ground, which cools and thickens as the night progresses. However, as cold air is denser than warm air, if your weather station is on a cliff all this cold air will simply slide downhill. This is why flat areas and especially the bottoms of canyons tend to be cool at night, as they can collect and hold that cold air.

Heatwaves!

When the ocean does finally lose its stranglehold on La Jolla's climate, then things get spicy! 100ºF is unfathomably hot, especially given how mild La Jolla's climate normally is. Another interesting quirk of the climate is that it can be sweltering any month of the year. Indeed, so far in 2021 the hottest temperature of the year was 85ºF, recorded on Januray 15th. We've gotten close in some of the recent November heatwaves, but still can't beat the heart of winter, which is quite humorous. The presence of such heatwaves in midwinter is often caused by the elimination of marine influence by seasonal Santa Ana winds.

Nearby Climates

To get a sense of just how variable the weather is within the San Diego area, let's look at some of NOAA's 1991-2020 normals for two nearby stations at Miramar and San Diego International airports

Interestingly, the daytime temperatures at San Diego International are quite a bit warmer than in La Jolla, with the averages differing by 3-5ºF. It is also somewhat warmer at night. This is unsurpirising as San Diego airport is further from the open ocean. Miramar, being around 5 miles inland from UCSD, is even warmer, because the oceanic influence there is weaker. In light of these normals nothing seems terribly wrong with the statistics I have calculated, which is good.

The other topic of note is the average rainfall, which is underwhelming at best. Although spatial variations in rainfall are present, they are not nearly as strong as those in temperature (as long as you stay away from the mountains), so these numbers should be representative of La Jolla's rainfall. No wonder the hillsides always look so dry!

What is UCSD's HDH doing?

This is a bit of a digression, but HDH's average temperatures for UCSD (available on page 3 of this FAQ packet) are confusing and seemingly in error. There isn't a citation either!!! They're just there.

These numbers do not match mine at all, being about 8-10ºF too warm in the day and 3-5ºF too cool at night. So the question is where did they get them from? They don't look like NOAA's normals for Miramar or San Diego International, so this mystery will for now have to remain unsolved.
